→

→

Used Claude to stress-test the UX: "What would a first-time parent find confusing here?" and refined accordingly
🎨
Google Fonts
Typography System
Used Fraunces (an optical-size serif with a warm, editorial character) paired with DM Sans (clean, functional, approachable) to create a design voice that feels premium but not cold — right for a family-oriented product.
→
Fraunces for all headlines, prices, and key UI moments — creates personality and warmth
→
DM Sans for body text, labels, and UI chrome — clean, readable at small sizes
🔍
Omnify Live Store Research
Competitive Benchmarking
Spent time in the example Service Store (uproboticstech.getomnify.com) to understand the current UX: the filter-heavy interface, form flows, and calendar interaction patterns that parents experience today.
→
Identified the core friction: parents must already know what they want before the interface is useful
→
Noted that there's no progressive disclosure — everything is shown at once, which overwhelms new users
→
Used this to sharpen the "AI-first" contrast in the Before/After marketing asset
💡
Design Principles (Personal Framework)
Aesthetic Direction
Applied a "warm editorial" aesthetic direction — influenced by premium parenting brands and concierge services — to ensure the design doesn't feel like generic SaaS but like something a parent would actually trust with their child's enrollment.
→
Palette: deep teal (trust, calm), coral (energy, action), warm cream (safety, warmth) — not typical SaaS blue/purple
→
AI is represented by ✦ (a subtle star/spark) rather than a robot icon — feels assistive, not intrusive
→
Language throughout uses "her", "Aria", "your child" — AI that remembers context feels like a real concierge
Key product decisions
Why a conversational prompt bar instead of filters?
Parents often don't know the vocabulary — "Level 2 Swim" means nothing if you've never enrolled before. A natural language prompt lets them say "my 7-year-old loves water but has never swum" and the AI translates that into structured recommendations. The filters remain available for power users, but they're not the entry point.
Why show a "match percentage" on cards?
It externalises the AI's reasoning in a way parents can quickly scan. 96% vs 79% tells a story without requiring them to read a full explanation. It also makes the top recommendation feel earned, not arbitrary.
Why include the Trial vs Membership modal?
This is the moment of highest anxiety for first-time parents — committing money without knowing if their child will enjoy it. An AI that proactively offers a clear comparison (with a verdict) at exactly that moment dramatically reduces abandonment and builds trust. It's AI earning its place in the UX, not AI for the sake of it.
Why pre-fill the registration form with AI context?
Every field a parent has to re-type is friction. If the AI already knows Aria is 7, a beginner, and interested in swimming from the conversation — the form should reflect that. This collapses a 5-minute form to a 30-second confirmation.
Time spent: Approximately 4.5 hours total. Breakdown: 45 min product thinking & UX mapping, 30 min aesthetic direction, 3 hrs implementation across 5 deliverables, 15 min copy refinement.

What I'd do with more time: Add a mobile viewport, build the payment step (Screen 4), create an animated prototype showing the AI typing in real-time, and test copy with actual parents.
